Many experiments at the Dr. Goard Research lab involve imaging the brains of mice while they play simple video games. One of the largest challenges with running these experiments is training the mice to play the games. I designed an automated training system that allows mice to play the video games at their leisure, and rewards them for their performance.
The mouse being trained is able to access this viewport from a hole in its cage. It uses the handlebar shaped joystick to play the game, and receives water from the spout upon success
Joystick input is read through an optical rotary encoder, and limits of rotation are set by two servo motors.
A UCSB experiment in the department of anthropology aims to test whether variations in performance between men and women in mental rotation tasks might have been an evolutionary trait that improved hunting ability in early humans. I designed a series of video games for this experiment to test participants ability to perform hunting related tasks.

The games can be downloaded here: Windows Installer (x64)
Source code can be found here: Github Link
One of the tasks requires participants to guess the landing location of a projectile.
The participants are also tested in their ability to quickly find camouflaged animals in various photos.
The results are compared to each participant's performance in the mental rotation task.
During my studies of thermodynamics I found myself repeatedly writing similar programs to solve different configurations of thermal networks. I wanted to create an all-encompassing method to solve thermal networks, so I created a matlab program to do just that. This tool can be used to model transient and equilibrium behaviour of heated objects, as well as approximate thermal resistance of complex parts.

Source code for the tool can be found here: Github Link
The user manual can be found here: userManual.pdf
As an example, here the tool is used to solve the equilibrium temperature of a motherboard while the CPU is generating heat and no cooler is attached.
The motherboard is modeled as a rectangular prism with the CPU as its only heat source. Heat is able to conduct accross the motherboard and convect to the surroundings via natural convection.
The code to run this simulation can be downloaded here: Download Link
