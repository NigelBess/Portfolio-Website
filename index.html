Experiments at the Dr. Goard Research lab involve imaging the brains of mice while they play simple video games. I joined the lab as a systems engineer and led a small team of undergraduate engineers to develop an automated device for training mice to play those games. We designed everything including hardware, software, electronics, robotics control, and data collection in a high-reliability and low-cost package capable of running 24 hours a day for months on end without human supervision.

The system yielded mice with higher in-game performance and reduced training times by as much as 80 percent. It also proved to be extremely scalable and led to a 600 percent increase in training capacity. These metrics were incredibly valuable to the lab, whose limiting factor in research was often their supply of trained mice.

Source code for the project can be found here: Games and Hardware Interfacing, Game Engine
Documentation for the game engine can be found here: Game Engine Docs

In June 2017 I began working on a realtime online MOBA-like game, called Wildy. The project has been a tremendous undertaking, as beyond requiring the basics of game development (architecture, 3D animation, GPU computing, etc), Wildy posed the additional challenge of maintaining a consistent game-state across multiple clients. This leadsto some complicated distributed systems and consensus problems which taught me many lessons in computing and software engineering. I was able to bring the game to a playable state and had a couple successful online play tests, but in the end, poor architecture choices led to an unmaintanable mess which would be excessively time-consuming to work on. While the game was never fully released it has taught me many lessons that have led to successes in multiple large-scale projects in the professional world.
